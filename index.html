About Our Lab

Our Mission

We are dedicated to advancing knowledge through innovative research and collaboration across disciplines. Our lab focuses on solving complex problems in computational biology, machine learning, and data science.

Founded in 2010, our research group has grown to include over 20 researchers working on cutting-edge projects with real-world applications.

Our Vision

We envision a future where data-driven approaches transform our understanding of biological systems and lead to breakthroughs in medicine, agriculture, and environmental science.

Our work bridges the gap between theoretical computer science and practical biological applications, creating tools and insights that benefit the scientific community and society at large.

Research Domains

Genomic Data Analysis

Developing algorithms for processing and interpreting large-scale genomic datasets to identify disease markers and biological insights.

Machine Learning

Applying advanced ML techniques to biological data for pattern recognition, prediction, and knowledge discovery.

Network Biology

Studying biological systems as complex networks to understand interactions and emergent properties.

Drug Discovery

Single-Cell Genomics

Our Team

Dr. Sarah Johnson

Lab Director & Principal Investigator

Dr. Johnson is a renowned expert in computational biology with over 15 years of research experience. She received her Ph.D. from Stanford University and has published over 80 peer-reviewed papers in top-tier journals. Her research focuses on developing novel algorithms for genomic data analysis and machine learning applications in biology.

Under her leadership, the research group has secured over $10M in funding from NIH, NSF, and private foundations. Dr. Johnson is a recipient of the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers (PECASE) and serves on the editorial boards of several prestigious journals.

Publications

Deep learning approaches for genomic variant calling from sequencing data

Johnson, S., Chen, M., & Rodriguez, E. (2023). Nature Methods, 20(3), 345-357.

This paper presents a novel deep learning framework that improves the accuracy of variant calling from next-generation sequencing data by 27% compared to existing methods.

PDF BibTeX
Network-based integration of multi-omics data for cancer subtype identification

Chen, M., Anderson, L., & Wilson, J. (2022). Cell Systems, 15(2), 125-139.

We developed a network-based approach that integrates genomic, transcriptomic, and proteomic data to identify novel cancer subtypes with distinct clinical outcomes.

PDF BibTeX
Transformer architectures for protein function prediction

Rodriguez, E., Kim, D., & Johnson, S. (2022). Bioinformatics, 38(5), 1321-1328.

This work adapts transformer architectures originally developed for natural language processing to predict protein function from sequence alone, achieving state-of-the-art performance.
